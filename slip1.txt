Q1. Steps to train bank staff to mitigate social engineering risks
Regular Security Awareness Training
Educate employees about phishing, vishing, baiting, and impersonation attacks.

Simulated Attacks (Phishing Drills)
Conduct mock phishing emails/calls to test employee response and improve awareness.

Strong Verification Procedures
Train staff to verify identity before sharing any sensitive customer data.

Clear Security Policies
Implement strict rules for handling confidential information and access control.

Password & Authentication Training
Promote strong passwords and use of Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA).

Incident Reporting Mechanism
Encourage employees to report suspicious activities immediately.

Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)
Limit access to sensitive data based on job roles.

Regular Updates & Workshops
Keep staff updated about new cyber threats and social engineering techniques.

Email & Communication Awareness
Teach employees to identify suspicious emails, links, and attachments.

Behavioral Training
Train staff to stay calm under pressure and avoid urgency-based manipulation.

Q2. Role of IT Act, 2000 in investigation and prosecution
The  plays a key role in cybercrime cases:

Legal Framework
Provides legal recognition to electronic transactions and cyber offences.

Defines Cyber Crimes
Sections like 43, 66, 66C, 66D deal with hacking, identity theft, and online fraud.

Punishment & Penalties
Specifies fines and imprisonment for cybercriminals.

Investigation Powers
Allows law enforcement agencies to investigate digital evidence.

Admissibility of Evidence
Recognizes electronic records as valid evidence in court.

Protection of Data
Ensures security and confidentiality of customer information.

Support for Prosecution
Helps in filing cases, prosecuting offenders, and delivering justice.
